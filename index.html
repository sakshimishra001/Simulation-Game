Simulation Guidelines

Purpose

This simulation places you in the role of Product Strategy Lead at Orion Mobiles. Your objective is to evaluate the company's position and determine how limited strategic resources should be allocated to improve performance.


Simulation Structure

  • Review the company background and market context
  • Analyze current performance indicators
  • Define strategic priorities and objectives
  • Allocate capital to potential initiatives
  • Review the resulting strategic performance report

Decision Principles

  • Engineering capacity and capital are limited
  • Strategic trade-offs are required
  • Prioritization should align with your declared strategy
  • Fragmentation of resources reduces effectiveness

Evaluation Overview

Your final performance will be assessed using multiple factors including strategic coherence, capital prioritization, and outcome projections. Decisions that demonstrate clear focus and disciplined allocation typically produce stronger results.


Important Notes

  • The simulation should be completed in one session.
  • Once submitted, strategic decisions cannot be changed.
  • Results are generated based on the consistency between your strategy and your allocation choices.

The Rise

Orion Mobiles was founded in 2002 by hardware engineers who believed operational discipline could outperform flashy innovation. And for over a decade, it did.

By 2014, Orion held 28% market share with strong margins and one of the highest customer retention rates in its segment. It wasn’t glamorous : it was reliable.

The Drift

Instead of choosing one direction, Orion chose many. R&D initiatives expanded from 3 to 11. Premium and value segments were pursued simultaneously.

Market share declined. Retention eroded. Margins compressed. Not from incompetence — from fragmentation.

“Orion does not suffer from lack of capability. It suffers from lack of prioritization.”
— Board Strategy Memo, Q4

Your Mandate


You are appointed as Product Strategy Lead. Not to invent new initiatives but to decide which ones deserve capital.

Engineering capacity is limited. Trade-offs are unavoidable. Coherence must be restored.
